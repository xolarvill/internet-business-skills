# Validation Lenses

Use this file to decide which claims belong in the idea-verification pass. Choose only the lenses needed to pressure-test the idea.

Every report must cover the baseline commercial viability dimensions somewhere in the output. If public evidence is unavailable for a dimension, mark it `unclear` and name the missing source type instead of dropping the dimension.

## Market Existence And Need Stability

Use when the idea assumes a market exists for durable reasons.

Look for:

- why the market exists
- the underlying job or pain behind current products
- stable substitutes buyers already use
- signs that demand is tied to a durable need, not only a short-lived product format

Weak shortcut:

- `This product category exists`

Stronger signal:

- buyers repeatedly describe the same job, pain, or outcome across products and channels

## Buyer Motivation

Use when the idea needs a clear reason someone would buy.

Look for:

- trigger moments
- reason to switch from substitutes
- willingness to pay for the promised outcome
- emotional, functional, or risk-reduction reason to act now

## Demand Signal

Use when the idea assumes real buyer interest exists.

Look for:

- visible search demand proxies
- active category pages and listings
- repeated public buying discussions
- clear pain-point language
- evidence of repeat or growing attention

Weak shortcut:

- `People talk about it online`

Stronger signal:

- repeated public buying intent, listings, and problem-specific discussion

## Audience Fit

Use when the idea assumes a specific buyer type will respond.

Look for:

- who brands in the category appear to target
- who reviews and community threads suggest is actually buying
- whether the proposed customer has a visible pain or desire

## Competitor Pressure

Use when the idea assumes room exists in the market.

Look for:

- number and type of visible competitors
- direct substitutes versus adjacent players
- whether the proposed position is obviously occupied
- whether incumbents are weak or strong on trust and proof

Competitor presence does not kill an idea by itself. It only weakens ideas that rely on being novel or lightly contested.

## Differentiation

Use when the idea assumes it can be meaningfully chosen over substitutes.

Look for:

- product-level differences
- proof or guarantee differences
- offer, bundle, or service-model differences
- channel-native positioning differences
- whether the difference is easy for a buyer to understand quickly

If the only difference is vague branding, mark the differentiation claim weak unless public evidence shows buyers value that brand angle.

## Channel Reality

Use when the idea assumes a specific acquisition or distribution path.

Look for:

- whether the category is active on that channel
- whether similar products visibly convert there
- whether the channel rewards the proposed price or content format
- platform restrictions or category frictions

## Acquisition Economics And CAC Proxy

Use when the idea depends on paid ads, marketplace discovery, influencers, affiliates, sales outreach, or SEO.

Look for:

- visible ad density or advertiser competition
- marketplace ranking pressure and sponsored-listing clutter
- SEO difficulty signals such as strong incumbents dominating informational and commercial queries
- whether the price point can plausibly absorb paid acquisition
- whether organic, referral, or repeat channels can reduce acquisition dependence

Do not invent CAC. Use public proxies and mark exact CAC as private or primary-data dependent.

## LTV Or Repeat-Purchase Plausibility

Use when the business needs repeat purchase, retention, subscription, upsell, refill, or account expansion.

Look for:

- consumable, replacement, refill, or subscription logic
- accessory or cross-sell paths
- review or community signals about repeat use
- churn, durability, or one-and-done purchase risk
- reasonable price and margin headroom for the expected purchase frequency

Do not invent LTV. State a plausible LTV shape only when the category mechanics support it.

## Content And Discoverability Fit

Use when growth depends on SEO, social content, photography, video, UGC, reviews, or AI-mediated discovery.

Look for:

- search-friendly questions, comparisons, and problem language
- demo-friendly or visually inspectable product benefits
- product photography and short-video suitability
- whether reviews can accumulate around specific proof points
- whether AI systems can understand, cite, or support the product from clear pages, FAQs, structured specs, and third-party mentions

## Trust And Proof Burden

Use when the product needs buyers to believe quality, safety, efficacy, or ROI claims.

Look for:

- review density
- guarantees
- demo-friendly proof
- expert validation
- whether trust is cheap or expensive to earn in this category

## Regulatory Or Policy Friction

Use when the idea touches health, claims, platform policy, or safety-sensitive categories.

Look for:

- obvious platform restrictions
- claim moderation patterns
- public policy documents
- community concern around compliance

## Operational Plausibility

Use only when public signals can say something useful.

Look for:

- fulfillment complexity visible in reviews
- obvious supply or margin warnings in category discussion
- shipping weight, fragility, size, hazmat, cold-chain, customs, or return friction
- complexity of explaining or onboarding the offer
- warranty, setup, compatibility, sizing, or customer-service burden

Do not fake deep operational certainty from shallow public data.

## Margin And Unit-Economics Plausibility

Use when the product has visible price bands, marketplace fees, heavy logistics, high returns, high support needs, or low willingness to pay.

Look for:

- public price bands
- obvious cost drivers such as materials, weight, size, breakage, certification, support, or returns
- discounting intensity
- whether the category appears able to support premium pricing

Do not claim exact margins without supplier quotes or internal data.

## After-Sales Drag

Use when post-purchase support could make the business harder than the sale.

Look for:

- return reasons
- warranty claims
- sizing, fit, compatibility, installation, calibration, or usage confusion
- safety, damage, refund, or replacement complaints
- whether poor support would quickly damage reviews

## Brand Compounding Potential

Use when the idea depends on more than one-off arbitrage.

Look for:

- memorable category position
- credible reason to expand into adjacent products
- trust, identity, expertise, mission, or community signals
- repeatable proof points that can become brand assets
- whether the brand would be legible to humans and AI systems over time
