# Research Rules

## Coverage Standard

Default to a useful audience portrait, not a fictional customer dossier. The analysis is stronger when it is supported by:

- primary brand or product pages
- multiple independent audience signals
- public reactions that reinforce or challenge brand intent
- visible reasons why a buyer would convert or hesitate

## Confidence Levels

Use these levels in the final report:

- `high`: multiple strong public signals support the audience cluster or motivation
- `medium`: some useful evidence exists, but coverage or signal quality is limited
- `low`: inference is thin, ambiguous, or weakly attributable

## Observed Signal Versus Hypothesis

Keep these labels distinct:

- `observed signal`: directly supported by visible language, behavior, reviews, or repeated public reactions
- `hypothesis`: a reasoned inference that fits the evidence but is not directly proven

Do not flatten them into the same confidence bucket.

## Audience Cluster Rules

Clusters should describe how buyers differ in commercially meaningful ways. Good clusters usually combine:

- what they are trying to do
- what they need to believe
- what they are sensitive to
- what would stop them

Good clusters:

- `performance-first owners who need proof of durability`
- `cautious first-time buyers seeking reassurance and clarity`
- `style-led gift shoppers who value presentation and simplicity`

Weak clusters:

- `young people`
- `pet lovers`
- `online shoppers`

## Brand Intent Versus Audience Response

Always ask whether the visible audience signals are:

- aligned with the brand's apparent target
- narrower than the brand's target
- broader than the brand's target
- in tension with the brand's target

This distinction is often more valuable than a flat persona summary.

## Commercial Judgment Layer

After clustering the audience, ask:

- Is the offer speaking clearly to the right people?
- Is proof strength matched to the audience's trust threshold?
- Is the audience problem one of clarity, credibility, or fit?
- Which cluster looks most commercially promising right now?

Good commercial judgments are:

- comparative between clusters
- grounded in visible evidence
- useful for positioning, merchandising, or messaging decisions

Examples:

- `The page appears to target premium, design-conscious buyers, but the visible proof is more likely to convince cautious practical buyers than true premium shoppers.`
- `The strongest audience fit is first-time buyers seeking reassurance, not expert users seeking technical differentiation.`
- `The offer looks broad on paper, but the evidence suggests the most responsive cluster is gift-oriented convenience buyers.`

Bad commercial judgments are:

- `The brand should target millennials.`
- `The audience is everyone.`
- `This product is for pet owners.`

## Honest Gaps

Always say when you could not verify:

- whether audience signals are current
- whether reviews reflect the same offer
- whether social reactions are representative
- whether the evidence supports behavior more than demographics
