# Audience Lenses

Use this file to decide what dimensions actually help portray the audience. Choose only the lenses that matter to the user's question.

## Use Case

Use when you need to understand what job the buyer is trying to get done.

Look for:

- everyday utility
- performance or expert use
- gifting
- first-time purchase
- premium self-reward
- convenience or time-saving

## Motivation

Use when the question is about why someone would buy.

Look for:

- safety or reliability
- status or taste
- convenience
- emotional reassurance
- value for money
- identity fit

## Trust Threshold

Use when conversion seems to depend on proof rather than raw interest.

Look for:

- review density
- guarantee or warranty signals
- materials proof
- expert or community validation
- visible before-and-after or usage proof

## Price Sensitivity

Use when the offer may appeal differently across price-conscious and premium-accepting buyers.

Look for:

- discount framing
- value bundles
- premium justification
- community comments about "worth it" versus "overpriced"

## Identity Signal

Use when buyers appear to care how the product reflects on them.

Look for:

- design-conscious language
- enthusiast or hobbyist signals
- giftability
- social or lifestyle framing
- mission or values alignment

## Objection Pattern

Use when you need to understand who will hesitate or bounce.

Look for:

- quality doubts
- complexity or setup friction
- mismatch with advanced use
- mismatch with beginner confidence
- price resistance

## Fit Versus Misfit

Use when the question is whether the product speaks clearly to one audience or awkwardly to several.

Look for:

- sharp audience clarity
- blended messaging
- premium cues with value proof gaps
- expert claims with beginner-oriented proof
- style-led framing with weak functional reassurance

## What To Avoid

Do not over-index on demographic labels if the public evidence is really about behavior, intent, or trust.

Weak audience portrayal:

- `women 25-34 who live in cities`
- `high-income millennials`

unless the evidence genuinely supports those claims.

Strong audience portrayal:

- `cautious first-time buyers seeking reassurance`
- `performance-oriented owners who prioritize reliability over style`
- `gift buyers attracted by presentation and simplicity`
